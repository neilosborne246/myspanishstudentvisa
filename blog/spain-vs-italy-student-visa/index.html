Spain vs Italy Student Visa: Which Is Right for You in 2026?

Spain and Italy are Europe's two most popular student visa destinations outside the UK. Both offer world-class education, great weather, and rich culture — but the visa processes, costs, and student experiences are quite different.

If you are deciding between studying in Spain or Italy, you are choosing between two of the most culturally rich, historically layered, and academically respected countries in Europe. Both sit within the Schengen Area. Both offer beautiful cities, Mediterranean climates, and full language immersion. Both require a long-stay student visa (Type D) for non-EU nationals studying for more than 90 days.

But when it comes to the practical realities — the visa process, the cost of living, work rights, processing times, and post-study options — Spain and Italy are more different than many applicants realise. This guide gives you an honest, detailed comparison across every dimension that matters.

At a Glance: Spain vs Italy for Students

Before diving into the detail, here is the headline comparison. Spain wins on process consistency, work rights, cost of living, and language reach. Italy wins on specific academic prestige (particularly in architecture, design, and fashion), and for students who specifically want to learn Italian and experience Italian culture. For most non-EU students making a practical decision, Spain is the more straightforward and affordable choice.

Bottom line: If you are weighing up Spain vs Italy primarily on the visa process, cost of living, and work rights — Spain is the stronger option on all three. If your decision is driven by specific academic programmes, cultural preference, or a passion for Italian — Italy may be worth the additional complexity.

Visa Requirements Compared

Both Spain and Italy require a long-stay student visa — the Type D visa — for non-EU nationals wishing to study for more than 90 days. The core requirements are similar in principle, but there are practical differences in how they are applied.

Spain's document requirements are more standardised. The Spanish consulate network publishes a consistent document checklist, and most consulates follow it reliably. The key documents are: the EX-00 application form, enrolment letter with weekly teaching hours and exact dates, criminal record certificate (apostilled and sworn-translated), medical certificate (referencing the 2005 International Health Regulations, sworn-translated), proof of financial means, health insurance, and proof of tuition fee payment.

Italy's requirements are similar but can vary more between consulates. The Italian student visa checklist is generally aligned across consulates, but there is more reported variation in what individual consulates ask for. The medical certificate is not always required for Italian student visa applications. Some Italian consulates request housing documents upfront; others do not. If applying for an Italian student visa, always check the current checklist with the specific consulate in your country.

Processing Times Compared

Processing times are one of the most significant practical differences between the two countries. Spain's student visa typically takes 4–8 weeks during peak season (April–August) and 2–4 weeks during low season. This is a relatively predictable window that allows applicants to plan their course start dates with reasonable certainty.

Italy's student visa processing times are generally longer and more variable. Four to twelve weeks is a typical range, but some applicants — particularly those applying during peak summer season — have reported waits of 3 months or more. For students with fixed course start dates, Italy's processing variability introduces meaningful planning risk.

If you have a fixed course start date and cannot afford to miss it — for example, a university semester beginning in September — Spain's more consistent 4–8 week processing timeline is significantly safer than Italy's 4–12 week range.

Cost of Living Compared: City by City

Spain is broadly more affordable than Italy for students, and this difference is most pronounced outside the capital cities. Spain's student-friendly cities — Seville, Granada, Valencia, and Malaga — are among the most affordable places to live in Western Europe. Italy's equivalent cities — Naples, Palermo, Bologna — are also affordable by European standards, but generally still pricier than Spanish equivalents.

Madrid vs Rome

Madrid and Rome are broadly comparable as capital cities. Both require a monthly budget of approximately €1,400–€1,900 for a student including accommodation, food, transport, and social expenses. Madrid is generally slightly more affordable than Rome, particularly for accommodation in student-friendly neighbourhoods.

Barcelona vs Milan

Barcelona is notably more affordable than Milan. Milan is one of Europe's most expensive cities — students typically need €1,600–€2,200 per month for a comfortable student life. Barcelona is expensive by Spanish standards but still more affordable than Milan at approximately €1,400–€1,900 per month.

Seville vs Naples

This is where Spain's affordability advantage is most pronounced. Seville is one of the most popular and affordable student cities in Europe — a monthly budget of €900–€1,200 is very comfortable. Naples is Italy's most affordable major city but still requires €1,100–€1,500 per month for a similar lifestyle.

Work Rights Compared

Spain allows student visa holders to work up to 30 hours per week — one of the most generous work allowances in the EU for student visa holders. This is a substantial benefit for students who want to offset living costs or gain work experience during their studies. You will need to register with Spanish social security and obtain a NIE (foreigner identification number) before working.

Italy permits student visa holders to work, but the allowance is typically up to 20 hours per week for standard study visa categories. The exact limit can vary depending on your specific visa type and the nature of your studies. Italy's work registration process is also more complex than Spain's, involving the questura and additional administrative steps.

For students who intend to work part-time during their studies, Spain's 30-hour allowance and simpler work registration process make it the clearly stronger option.

Language Learning Value

Both Spanish and Italian are beautiful, expressive Romance languages — and studying in either country offers genuine full immersion. The question is what you want to do with the language after you leave.

Spanish is spoken by approximately 500 million people and is the official language of 21 countries across Latin America, Europe, and Africa. It is consistently rated as one of the most valuable languages for international business, and its reach across the Americas makes it particularly valuable for applicants from English-speaking countries. Spanish is also rated as one of the easiest languages for English speakers to learn.

Italian is spoken by approximately 85 million people, primarily in Italy and Switzerland. It is a prestige language in fashion, opera, art, and food — and a wonderful language to speak. However, its global business utility is significantly narrower than Spanish. If language learning value and global reach are key factors in your decision, Spanish wins clearly.

University Rankings and Academic Prestige

Both Spain and Italy have strong higher education systems with internationally recognised universities. The right choice depends heavily on your field of study.

Spain's strongest institutions: IE University (consistently ranked among Europe's top business schools), IESE Business School, ESADE, Universidad Carlos III de Madrid, Universitat Pompeu Fabra, and University of Navarra. Spain is particularly strong in business, law, and the social sciences.

Italy's strongest institutions: Politecnico di Milano (architecture and engineering — top 10 globally), Bocconi University (business and economics — top 20 globally), University of Bologna (one of the world's oldest universities, strong across many disciplines), and University of Padua. Italy is particularly strong in architecture, design, engineering, and fashion.

If your target programme is at one of Italy's world-leading institutions in architecture or design — Politecnico di Milano ranks consistently in the global top 10 for architecture — the visa complexity is worth it. For most other fields, Spain offers comparable or superior options with fewer application headaches.

Renewing Your Student Visa: Spain vs Italy

One aspect of the student visa experience that many applicants overlook at the application stage is the renewal process. Both Spain and Italy allow students to extend or renew their student status while in-country — but the experience is very different.

In Spain, students apply for a TIE card (Tarjeta de Identidad de Extranjero) within 30 days of arriving, and renew it at the local extranjería office as needed. The process, while not without bureaucratic friction, is well-understood and manageable. Many immigration specialists — including Platinum Legal Spain — can assist with TIE renewals and extensions.

In Italy, the student permit of stay (permesso di soggiorno per motivi di studio) is processed through the post office (Sportello Amico) and then the questura (police headquarters). The Italian permesso di soggiorno process is widely reported as slower and more bureaucratically demanding than Spain's TIE equivalent. Delays of several months are not uncommon.

Which Is Better for Language School Students?

For students coming specifically to learn the language at a language school — rather than pursue a degree or postgraduate qualification — Spain is the stronger choice on almost every metric. Spanish language schools are numerous, well-regulated, and affordable. Course costs at reputable Dele-accredited schools start from approximately €300–€500 per month for intensive courses. Major cities like Madrid, Seville, and Barcelona all have large, thriving language school communities with dedicated international student infrastructure.

Italian language schools are available but less numerous and generally more expensive. For students who specifically want to learn Italian — particularly in immersive settings like Florence, Siena, or Perugia — an Italian language school offers a wonderful experience. But in terms of practical value, language accessibility, and overall cost, Spain is the stronger proposition.

Which Is Better for Degree Students?

For undergraduate degree students, both countries offer a wide range of programmes. Spanish public universities are more affordable than Italian equivalents for EU and non-EU students, with tuition fees typically in the range of €1,000–€3,500 per year depending on the institution and programme. Italian public universities are also generally affordable — fees of €1,000–€3,000 are typical — but fees at prestigious private institutions (Bocconi, LUISS) are significantly higher.

For postgraduate students, Spain has a particularly strong offering at the Masters level — especially in business. IE University, IESE, and ESADE consistently produce globally competitive graduates and are sought after by international employers. If business education is your goal, Spain has the edge.

Final Verdict: Spain or Italy?

For the majority of non-EU international students, Spain is the more practical, affordable, and accessible choice. The student visa process is more consistent, processing times are more predictable, the cost of living is lower, work rights are more generous, and Spanish language skills have greater global utility.

Italy is the right choice if your academic goal specifically requires an Italian institution — particularly in architecture (Politecnico di Milano), fashion (Istituto Marangoni), or business (Bocconi) — or if learning Italian and experiencing Italian culture is a core part of your study plan.

For everyone else, Spain offers everything Italy does — great weather, rich culture, world-class food, European adventure, language immersion — with a simpler process, lower costs, and broader career value from the language you will learn.
