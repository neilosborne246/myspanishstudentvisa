Spain vs Portugal Student Visa: The Honest 2026 Comparison

Spain and Portugal are Iberian neighbours with similar cultures but genuinely different visa processes, costs, and long-term prospects for international students.

Start Your Application ← Comparisons Hub
Home›Blog›Spain vs Portugal

Spain and Portugal are neighbours on the Iberian Peninsula and share many cultural similarities — yet for international students, the two countries offer meaningfully different experiences. Both are popular study destinations, both are in the Schengen Area, and both use the euro. But the visa processes, financial thresholds, English-language provision, tuition costs, and post-study pathways have important differences. This guide breaks down the key factors side by side so you can make the right choice for your situation and goals.

Visa Process: Spain vs Portugal

Both countries issue long-stay student visas, but the administrative processes differ in important ways:

  • ('h3', 'Spain: Estancia por Estudios (Type D)')
  • Processed at the Spanish consulate in your country of residence. No pre-application screening body required. Financial threshold based on IPREM — approximately €600–€900/month. Processing typically 4–12 weeks. Health insurance required with no co-payments and minimum €30,000 coverage. Background check apostilled and sworn-translated required.
  • ('h3', 'Portugal: Visto de Estudo (Type D)')
  • Processed at the Portuguese consulate. Portugal has streamlined its student visa process significantly in recent years — it is generally considered one of the more straightforward EU student visa systems. Processing typically 4–8 weeks in most countries. Financial threshold approximately €760/month. Criminal record check required. Health insurance required.
  • ('h3', 'Key Differences')
  • Spain's IPREM-based threshold (€600/month) is lower than Portugal's reference (€760/month), potentially making Spain easier to demonstrate financial means for lower-budget applicants. Portugal's processing times are generally slightly faster.

Tuition Fees: Spain vs Portugal

Tuition costs differ significantly between the two countries, particularly at public universities:

  • Spain public universities (non-EU students): €5,000–€12,000/year for most undergraduate programmes; master's degrees vary widely.
  • Portugal public universities (non-EU students): approximately €3,500–€7,500/year — generally cheaper than Spanish public universities.
  • Private language schools: broadly comparable in both countries — €3,000–€8,000 for intensive annual language programmes.
  • Business and management schools: Spain has stronger private business schools by global ranking (IE, IESE, ESADE) with correspondingly higher fees.
  • For students targeting public university undergraduate programmes: Portugal offers a significant cost advantage over Spain.

Cost of Living: Spain vs Portugal

Portugal — particularly Lisbon — has experienced rapid cost-of-living increases in recent years, significantly narrowing the gap with Spain:

  • Lisbon: monthly living costs €1,000–€1,600/month all-in (flat, food, transport, social).
  • Porto: €850–€1,300/month.
  • Smaller Portuguese cities (Coimbra, Braga, Évora, Aveiro): €700–€1,000/month.
  • Madrid and Barcelona: €1,200–€2,000/month.
  • Mid-sized Spanish cities (Valencia, Seville, Bilbao): €900–€1,400/month.
  • Smaller Spanish cities (Salamanca, Granada, Murcia): €700–€1,000/month.
  • Bottom line: in smaller cities, the cost difference between Spain and Portugal is minimal. Lisbon is now broadly comparable to mid-sized Spanish cities.

English-Language Study Options

Both countries have grown their English-language academic provision significantly:

  • Spain: a large and growing number of private universities and business schools offer full English-medium programmes, particularly at postgraduate level. Language schools are extensive. Public universities are largely Spanish-medium with some international master's programmes taught in English.
  • Portugal: many postgraduate programmes at Portuguese public universities are now taught in English — particularly at Nova University Lisbon and the University of Lisbon. Undergraduate provision remains largely in Portuguese.
  • For students wanting to study without first learning the local language: Spain has more total English-medium programmes due to its larger size and more developed private university sector.

Work Rights Comparison

Both countries allow students to work part-time, but the permitted hours differ:

  • Spain: up to 30 hours per week during term time, full-time during official academic holiday periods.
  • Portugal: up to 20 hours per week during term time, full-time during official holidays.
  • Spain's higher weekly limit is a meaningful advantage for students who need or want to work more to offset costs.
  • Both countries allow students to participate in curricular internships (prácticas curriculares in Spain, estágios in Portugal).
  • Both countries restrict employment to activities compatible with student status.

Academic Quality and Rankings

Both countries have internationally respected universities but at different levels in global rankings:

  • Top Spanish universities: Complutense de Madrid, Universitat de Barcelona, Autónoma de Madrid, and IE/IESE/ESADE business schools. IE and IESE consistently rank in the European top 3 for MBA programmes.
  • Top Portuguese universities: University of Lisbon, Nova University Lisbon, University of Porto, and Universidade Católica Portuguesa.
  • By global rankings: Spanish universities tend to score higher in research output in several fields (physics, medicine, engineering). Portuguese universities have punched above their weight in recent years.
  • For language learning: Spain's own language qualification network (DELE from Instituto Cervantes) is globally recognised. Portugal's CAPLE (Certificado de Português como Língua Estrangeira) is similarly respected.

Post-Study Pathways: Spain vs Portugal

Both countries offer post-graduation job seeker visas and have growing economies:

  • ('h3', 'Spain Post-Study Options')
  • Spain offers a búsqueda de empleo visa giving 12 months to find employment after graduation. Spain's digital nomad visa requires €2,100/month income and offers favourable tax treatment under the Beckham Law (flat 24% income tax rate). Spain's labour market is larger — GDP roughly 4x Portugal's.
  • ('h3', 'Portugal Post-Study Options')
  • Portugal offers a procura de emprego job seeker visa (12 months). Portugal's IFICI tax regime (successor to NHR) offers reduced tax rates for qualifying new residents. Lisbon's tech startup ecosystem has grown rapidly — Websummit is permanently based there. Brazil-linked business is a unique advantage for Portuguese speakers.

Language Learning: Spanish vs Portuguese

Both Spanish and Portuguese are excellent choices for language learners — but they serve different global markets:

  • Spanish: the world's second-most-spoken native language (480 million speakers). Official language of 20 countries across 3 continents. Growing rapidly in US markets — already spoken by 40+ million Americans.
  • Portuguese: the world's sixth-most-spoken language (250+ million speakers). Brazil alone — the world's ninth-largest economy — has 215 million Portuguese speakers. Also official in Angola, Mozambique, Cape Verde, and other African nations.
  • Spanish and Portuguese are mutually intelligible at a basic level — learning one makes learning the other significantly easier.
  • For maximum geographic reach and employability globally: Spanish has a slight edge. For access to Brazil and Lusophone Africa: Portuguese is the gateway.

Quality of Life: Spain vs Portugal

Quality of life is personal, but some objective differences are worth noting:

  • Climate: both countries have excellent Mediterranean/Atlantic climates. Portugal's Atlantic west coast is windier and wetter than Spain's Mediterranean coast. Algarve (Portugal) and Costa del Sol (Spain) are both globally popular for sunshine.
  • Food: both countries have world-class food cultures. Spain has more Michelin stars per capita. Portugal's seafood culture (bacalhau, grilled fish, pastéis de nata) is extraordinary.
  • Safety: both countries are genuinely safe. Portugal consistently ranks slightly higher in global safety indices than Spain.
  • Social culture: similar in many ways — late evenings, outdoor café culture, warm social norms. Portugal is considered slightly more reserved than Spain.
  • Bureaucracy: both countries have reputations for administrative complexity — but Portugal has invested more in digital government services in recent years and is sometimes considered slightly more efficient.
