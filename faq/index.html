Application & Eligibility

Non-EU/EEA nationals who want to study in Spain for more than 90 days need a student visa (estancia por estudios or visado de estudios). EU/EEA citizens do not require a visa and can enrol at any Spanish institution directly. If you hold an EU passport — even dual nationality — you travel and register as an EU citizen.
To qualify, a course must run for more than 90 days, require at least 20 hours of in-person classroom instruction per week, and be delivered by an accredited Spanish institution. Language schools, university degrees (grado), master's programmes, PhD programmes, and vocational training (Formación Profesional) all qualify. Online-only courses do not qualify for the student visa — in-person attendance is a hard requirement.
Yes — American citizens are non-EU nationals and need a student visa to study in Spain for more than 90 days. Spanish consulates in the US (Miami, Los Angeles, New York, Houston, Chicago, and San Francisco) are among the fastest globally, frequently processing applications in 1–3 weeks after the consulate appointment. You apply at the consulate serving your state of legal residence.
Yes — post-Brexit, British citizens are non-EU nationals and require the full Spain student visa for stays over 90 days. The application process is identical to that of other non-EU nationals. Apply at the Spanish Consulate in London (covering England, Wales, and Northern Ireland) or Edinburgh (covering Scotland). Processing at UK consulates typically takes 4–8 weeks after appointment.
Yes — up to 30 hours per week. The right to work is automatic with the student visa under Spanish law — no separate work permit is required. The 30-hour limit applies during term time; there is no restriction during official holiday periods. Your employer will see your TIE card as proof of your right to work.
Yes — spouses, registered civil partners, and dependent minor children can apply as accompanying family members (reagrupación familiar). Each family member requires their own individual application with their own documents and consulate appointment. Additional financial requirements apply for each dependent. Contact us for family pricing and full details.
Yes — the Spain student visa (tipo D, issued with MULT endorsement) permits free travel throughout the 27 Schengen member states for up to 90 days in any 180-day period outside Spain. Once your TIE card is issued in Spain, it also functions as a Schengen travel document alongside your passport. Keep your TIE with you when crossing borders.
The student visa (estancia por estudios) is for those enrolled in an official academic programme at an accredited institution. The researcher visa is for researchers formally affiliated with a recognised Spanish research organisation under a hosting agreement. Most PhD students at universities use the student visa — only PhD candidates working under a specific research hosting agreement would use the researcher route. If in doubt, confirm with your institution which category applies.

If you prefer to apply independently, you must do so through the Spanish consulate serving your country of legal residence. An in-person appointment at the consulate is mandatory — the visa cannot be obtained remotely.

Documents & Requirements

The core document list is: valid passport (minimum 12 months' validity beyond your intended departure from Spain), completed Spanish national visa application form (EX-01 or consulate-specific form), two recent passport photographs (35mm x 45mm, white background), proof of enrolment from an accredited Spanish institution, police clearance certificate from your country of residence with apostille, medical certificate from a registered doctor confirming no contagious diseases, private health insurance certificate, proof of accommodation in Spain, and bank statements demonstrating sufficient funds. Exact requirements can vary slightly by consulate.
An apostille is an official authentication certificate attached to a document, issued under the 1961 Hague Apostille Convention, confirming that the document is genuine and the issuing official's signature is authentic. Your police clearance certificate (criminal record) must have an apostille. Some consulates also require apostilles on birth certificates or marriage certificates if you're bringing family. If your country is not a Hague Convention signatory, a full diplomatic legalisation chain is required instead — which takes considerably longer.
Documents not issued in Spanish must be accompanied by a sworn translation (traducción jurada) into Spanish. This must be prepared by a translator officially sworn by the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs — a standard translation agency is not sufficient. This typically applies to your criminal record certificate and bank statements. Some consulates accept English documents without translation — confirm with your specific consulate.
Your police clearance certificate must be issued within the 3 months immediately prior to your consulate appointment. It then needs to be apostilled, which adds time. If your certificate is from a country with a slow apostille service, plan to request it well in advance and track the validity window carefully — an expired certificate will result in your application being rejected.
A signed rental contract or official letter of accommodation from a Spanish landlord or institution. Student halls of residence acceptance letters are acceptable. If staying with a Spanish resident, a formal signed letter of invitation (carta de invitación) from that person is required. The address must be in Spain — a hotel reservation is generally not accepted for a long-term student visa.
A signed certificate from a registered medical doctor confirming that you do not suffer from any diseases with serious public health implications as defined by the International Health Regulations 2005. This is a standard letter that most GPs or family doctors can issue. It does not require tests — it is a declaration of fitness. It must also be within 3 months of your appointment at some consulates.
It must be an official letter on headed paper from the institution, confirming your name, course name, course start and end dates, weekly contact hours (must show at least 20 hours in person per week), and the institution's official stamp or signature. If the letter is in English, check whether your consulate requires a sworn Spanish translation. We advise clients to request letters specifically worded to confirm the 20-hour attendance requirement.
Yes — savings are acceptable as proof of funds. Consulates look at the totality of your financial situation across 3–6 months of bank statements. A lump sum that arrived in your account immediately before the application is less convincing than savings that have built up over time. A combination of steady income (salary, rental income, regular transfers) plus a solid savings balance is the strongest profile.
Yes — parental or third-party sponsorship is widely accepted, particularly for younger students. You will need to provide the sponsor's bank statements and a signed letter of financial commitment from the sponsor confirming they will fund your studies and living costs in Spain. Some consulates ask for a notarised or witnessed version of this letter.

Timeline & Processing

The full process typically takes 8–12 weeks from starting document preparation to receiving your visa. Start at least 10 weeks before your intended travel date — 14 weeks if applying in peak season (May–August) when consulate appointment availability is lower. Document preparation alone can take 4–6 weeks depending on how quickly your criminal record and apostille can be obtained.
Once the consulate has your complete application, Spanish law requires a decision within one calendar month. In practice, US consulates often decide within 1–3 weeks; UK consulates typically take 4–6 weeks; some other consulates may use the full month. The bottleneck is usually getting a consulate appointment rather than the decision itself — popular consulates book up months in advance.
Most consulates will not accept a visa application more than 90 days before your course start date. The practical sweet spot is to book your consulate appointment 60–80 days before your course begins. This gives the consulate enough time to decide while keeping your appointment within the acceptable window. Do not apply too early — your application may be rejected or held.
If your consulate appointment is unavailable within a reasonable timeframe, contact the consulate directly to explain your course start date. Some consulates have urgent appointment slots for students with imminent start dates. In extreme cases, we can assist with letters to the consulate documenting your situation. Some institutions will also provide a brief grace period for enrolment — contact your school to confirm their policy.
The Spain student visa is valid from a specified start date and typically allows entry within a window printed on the visa sticker. You must enter Spain before the validity end date on the visa — failure to do so means the visa expires and you must reapply. Once you enter Spain, you have 30 days to register at the town hall (empadronamiento) and book your TIE appointment.
You have the right to lodge an administrative appeal (recurso de alzada) within one calendar month of the refusal notice date. The appeal goes to the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs in Madrid. Alternatively, you can reapply from scratch with a corrected and strengthened application. Common refusal reasons include insufficient financial evidence, incomplete documents, or a weakly worded enrolment letter. Our team can review a refusal and advise on the strongest path forward.
Yes. Your visa sticker in your passport is your authorisation to enter Spain and begin your studies. The TIE is applied for after arrival, not before. You enter on your visa, then apply for the TIE within 30 days. During the period between applying for your TIE and collecting it (which can take several weeks), your visa appointment confirmation receipt serves as your proof of status.
TIE production can take 3–8 weeks after your appointment. During this time your visa (if still valid) and your appointment receipt are your documents. If your original visa has expired and your TIE has not arrived, your EX-17 application form with its payment receipt is your legal proof of pending status. You can travel within Spain freely. Schengen travel without your TIE is more complex — seek advice before travelling internationally.

Health Insurance

Private health insurance from a company registered and operating in Spain. The policy must provide: no co-payments, no deductibles or excess charges, minimum €30,000 medical coverage per incident, coverage valid across all of Spain, and valid for the full duration of your course. International travel insurance policies are generally not accepted — it must be a Spanish-registered policy.
No. The EHIC/GHIC is only available to EU/EEA citizens and does not satisfy the visa insurance requirement. Non-EU nationals applying for the Spain student visa must have a Spanish-registered private insurance policy. Even EU citizens living in Spain long-term are generally advised to obtain local health registration rather than relying on EHIC for extended stays.
The major accepted providers include Asisa, Mapfre Salud, Sanitas (part of Bupa), AXA Spain, Cigna Spain, Adeslas, and DKV. Our partner site Spanish Health Insurance offers policies specifically designed for student visa applicants, with certificates worded to satisfy consulate requirements. Expect to pay €50–120/month for individual adult coverage.
The certificate must explicitly state: your full name, the policy period (covering your entire course duration), that coverage is provided across all of Spain, that there are no co-payments or deductibles, and the coverage limit (at least €30,000). The insurer must be identifiable as a Spanish-registered entity. Generic confirmation letters or policy summaries without these details are regularly rejected at consulate stage.
Yes. The tarjeta sanitaria gives you access to Spain's public health system for day-to-day care — but private insurance remains a legal condition of your student visa for its entire duration. You must maintain valid private insurance throughout your studies, even if you have full public health access. At renewal time, consulates check that your insurance has been continuous.
Allowing your health insurance to lapse is a breach of your visa conditions. At renewal, the extrajería office will check insurance continuity — a gap in coverage is one of the most common reasons for renewal difficulties. Set a calendar reminder well before expiry and renew at least 2 weeks before your current policy ends to ensure continuous coverage and to have the new certificate ready if required.
Family or couples policies are available and can be cost-effective. However, the certificate must list each person individually and confirm that each person has the required minimum coverage. Some consulates insist on separate certificates per person even if covered under one policy. We recommend obtaining individual certificates for each applicant to avoid any ambiguity during the consulate review.
It depends on the specific scheme. Some Spanish university insurance schemes meet the visa requirements; many do not — especially those offered by international or language schools. Check carefully that the scheme provides coverage of at least €30,000 with no co-payments or deductibles. Get a certificate from the scheme confirming all required terms explicitly. If in doubt, arrange a separate standalone policy to avoid any risk of rejection.

Financial Requirements

Bank statements covering the last 3–6 months should show approximately €600–800 per month for your stay. For a 12-month course, you should demonstrate at least €8,400 in sustained available funds, plus evidence that tuition fees are covered. The exact amount is not fixed in law — consulates assess the overall picture, but consistent monthly balances above €700 with no prolonged near-zero periods present the strongest case.
Not strictly — but the consulate will assess whether your demonstrated income and savings pattern makes it plausible you can sustain the full stay. A student with €3,000 in savings applying for a 2-year course will face more scrutiny than one with €20,000. Regular monthly income (salary, pension, parental support) that demonstrably covers ongoing costs is weighted positively. A large one-off deposit just before the application is viewed with scepticism.
Bank statements in any currency are acceptable provided the euro equivalent is clearly calculable. For non-euro statements, consulates will convert to euros at the current exchange rate. If your currency is volatile, a sworn translation of the statements accompanied by a note on exchange rates helps clarify the figures. If your bank provides euro statements alongside your local currency, include both.
Investment account statements, brokerage statements, and pension fund statements can be submitted as supplementary evidence of financial means. However, consulates prefer to see liquid bank account funds rather than illiquid investments. If your wealth is primarily in investments, provide a recent portfolio valuation statement alongside your bank statements and consider liquidating a portion into your bank account to demonstrate accessible funds.
No — you do not need a Spanish bank account to apply. Your home country bank statements are what the consulate reviews. You will, however, want to open a Spanish bank account relatively quickly after arrival in Spain — it is needed to pay your TIE fee (Tasa 790 código 012) at a Spanish bank, and useful for paying rent, utilities, and daily expenses. Popular options for new arrivals include BBVA, Sabadell, and digital options like Revolut or Wise.
Yes. If you have already paid tuition fees, include the payment receipt from your institution as supporting evidence. This reduces the amount of living funds you need to demonstrate, as the consulate is assessing your ability to cover all costs of your stay. A paid tuition receipt is particularly useful if your bank balance is lower than ideal — it removes a major expense from the equation.
If you work in Spain, you are subject to Spanish income tax on your Spanish earnings regardless of your visa type. Employers deduct income tax (IRPF) at source. If you earn below the annual personal allowance (approximately €15,000/year for a non-resident student), you may owe no tax and can recover withheld amounts via a tax return. Consult a gestor (Spanish tax administrator) for advice specific to your situation, especially if you have income from multiple countries.
Yes — financial requirements increase for each dependent. As a rough guide: student alone requires approximately €600–800/month; add approximately €150–250/month per dependent child and approximately €300–500/month for a spouse or partner. These thresholds are not fixed in legislation but reflect the IPREM-based assessments used in practice. Provide 6 months of bank statements rather than 3 when bringing dependents.

After Arrival in Spain

Within 30 days of arriving: (1) register at your local town hall (empadronamiento) — bring passport and lease; (2) book your TIE appointment online via the cita previa system — book within the first 3–5 days as slots fill quickly; (3) pay your TIE application fee (Tasa 790 código 012) at a Spanish bank; (4) attend your TIE appointment with form EX-17, payment receipt, passport, empadronamiento certificate, enrolment letter, insurance certificate, and passport photos. Register at your school or university once settled.
The Tarjeta de Identidad de Extranjero (TIE) is your physical Spanish residency card. It carries your NIE (Número de Identidad de Extranjero — your unique Spanish tax and identification number) and is proof of your legal student resident status. Apply for it at your local Oficina de Extrajería within 30 days of arriving in Spain using form EX-17. The card typically arrives 3–8 weeks after your appointment, during which your appointment receipt serves as proof of status.
Empadronamiento is registering your residential address on Spain's municipal census (padrón municipal) at your local ayuntamiento (town hall). It is legally required within 30 days of establishing residence. Beyond the legal obligation, you need the empadronamiento certificate for your TIE appointment, to register with a health centre, to enrol children in school, and for many other administrative processes. It is free and typically takes under 30 minutes in person.
No — the renewal (prórroga de estancia por estudios) is handled entirely within Spain at your local Oficina de Extrajería. You do not need to return to your home country or visit an embassy. The renewed TIE is issued in Spain. This is a major advantage of the student visa over some other visa categories — you can complete your full multi-year study programme without leaving Spain for immigration purposes.
Apply within the 60-day window before your current TIE card expires. Do not apply earlier — extrajería offices will generally not process the application before the 60-day window opens. Start assembling your renewal documents 8 weeks before your TIE expiry so you are ready to submit on the first eligible day. Common renewal documents: new insurance certificate, updated empadronamiento, proof of continued enrolment, and updated bank statements.
There is no fixed limit on the number of renewals. You can renew annually for as long as you remain enrolled in an eligible course and continue to meet all renewal conditions (valid insurance, sufficient funds, continued academic progression). After 5 years of continuous legal residence in Spain you may be eligible for long-term EU residency status — combined student and other legal residence periods count.
Options include: (1) the job seeker extension (prórroga para búsqueda de empleo) available for university graduates — gives 12 months to find sponsored employment; (2) a work permit sponsored by a Spanish employer; (3) transitioning to another visa type such as the Non-Lucrative Visa or Digital Nomad Visa if eligible; (4) continuing to further study. You must take action before your TIE expires — simply remaining in Spain after expiry is an immigration violation with serious consequences.
The Número de Identidad de Extranjero (NIE) is your unique personal identification number in Spain, used for all official and tax purposes. It is printed on your TIE card. You will need it to open a bank account, sign a rental contract, start employment, pay taxes, buy a vehicle, sign any official contract, and many other administrative tasks. Your NIE number never changes — it stays with you regardless of your visa status or how many times you renew.
Once you have your TIE and empadronamiento, you can register at your local health centre (centro de salud) and request a tarjeta sanitaria (health card) and assignment of a médico de cabecera (GP). This gives you access to Spain's public health system for routine care, prescriptions, and referrals. This registration does not replace your private insurance (which remains a visa condition) but means you have access to both systems.
