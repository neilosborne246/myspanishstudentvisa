Why We Built These Tools

The Misinformation Problem

The internet is full of outdated, inaccurate, and jurisdiction-specific advice about the Spain student visa that has been copied and re-published without verification. Blog posts from 2019 still rank highly in search results despite describing a requirements landscape that has since changed. The result is applicants arriving at consulate appointments with incomplete or incorrectly formatted documents — and being sent away. These tools are maintained by lawyers who handle live applications every week. When requirements change, the tools change. You can cross-reference our guidance against the official requirements page and the official Spanish extranjería portal to verify everything you read here.

The Cost of Errors

A refused student visa is not just frustrating — it is expensive. Course fees may be non-refundable. Flights and accommodation arrangements may be lost. Criminal record certificates need to be re-obtained. Sworn translations must be re-commissioned. The appointment cycle at busy consulates can add six to twelve weeks to your timeline. Our process guide explains the full application timeline, and these tools are designed to prevent the most common errors before they happen. We built them because we saw the same avoidable mistakes causing refusals — and we wanted applicants to have better information before they even picked up the phone to a lawyer.

Common Errors

Most Common Application Mistakes — and How These Tools Help Prevent Them

These are the seven errors our immigration lawyers see most frequently. Each one is a preventable cause of delay or refusal.

1

Applying at the Wrong Consulate

Spain student visas must be applied for at the Spanish consulate with jurisdiction over your place of legal residence — not the nearest consulate, and not the consulate in your home country if you live abroad. Our Consulate Finder eliminates this error by mapping your residence country to the correct consulate and flagging any additional local requirements.

2

Insufficient Financial Proof

Many applicants underestimate the financial threshold or submit bank statements that do not cover the required period (typically 3–6 months), do not carry a bank stamp, or show insufficient average balances. Our Financial Calculator tells you exactly how much you need to demonstrate and for how long, based on your course duration and number of dependants.

3

Wrong Type of Health Insurance

Travel insurance is consistently mistaken for the private health insurance required for the Spain student visa. The two are entirely different products. The visa requires a Spanish-market policy with no copayment, no deductible, minimum €30,000 coverage, and full territorial coverage of Spain. Our Document Checklist specifies exactly what the policy certificate must state.

4

Expired Criminal Record Certificate

Criminal record certificates must typically be no older than three months at the time of your consulate appointment. Processing times for certificates from countries like the USA (FBI), Australia (AFP), and Canada (RCMP) can run to several months. Applicants who order their certificate too late arrive with an expired document. The checklist includes processing time notes to help you plan.

5

Missing Apostille

Criminal record certificates must be apostilled by the competent authority in the issuing country before they are accepted by Spanish consulates. Many applicants submit unapostilled certificates and are refused. The apostille and sworn translation are two separate requirements — both must be present on the same document.

6

EX-00 Form Completed in English

The EX-00 form (Solicitud de autorización de estancia por estudios) must be completed in Spanish. A form completed in English is one of the most common — and most easily avoidable — grounds for rejection. It is a straightforward requirement that applicants frequently overlook because the form is available in English online. Our Document Checklist flags this explicitly.

7

Documents Not in Correct Format

Spanish consulates require original documents for most categories, certified copies for some, and sworn Spanish translations for any document not already in Spanish. Uncertified photocopies, informal translations by bilingual friends, and digital PDFs submitted instead of originals are common grounds for rejection. The checklist specifies the exact format required for each document category.
