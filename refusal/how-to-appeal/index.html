How to Appeal a Spain Student Visa Refusal — Your Rights and Options

A refusal is devastating — we understand that. But it is also a legal decision that you have the right to challenge. Here is exactly what your options are, how the process works, and what your realistic chances look like.

If your Spain student visa has been refused, you will feel frustrated, confused, and possibly desperate — especially if you have already paid tuition fees, booked flights, or given up a job. These are entirely understandable feelings. What matters now is making a clear-headed decision about what to do next.

Under Spanish administrative law, you have a right to challenge a visa refusal. There are two formal avenues: an administrative appeal (recurso de alzada) and, if that fails, a judicial appeal (recurso contencioso-administrativo). There is also the option of simply reapplying with a corrected application — which, depending on the reason for your refusal, may be faster and more practical than appealing.

Act fast. The deadline for an administrative appeal is one calendar month from the date of the refusal notification. This is not one month from when you read it — it is one month from the official notification date. Do not delay in getting legal advice.

The recurso de alzada is an administrative appeal submitted to the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs (specifically, to the body hierarchically superior to the consulate that issued the refusal — in practice, the central consular division of the Ministry). It is governed by Articles 121–122 of the Spanish Administrative Procedure Act (Ley 39/2015, de 1 de octubre).

You do not need a lawyer to file a recurso de alzada — it can be submitted by the applicant directly. However, in practice, a well-drafted appeal by an experienced immigration lawyer significantly increases the chance of a positive outcome. The appeal must be in Spanish, clearly identify the refusal decision being challenged (by reference to its date and the article of law cited), set out the grounds for the challenge, and request a specific outcome (typically: annulment of the refusal and grant of the visa, or alternatively, re-examination of the application).

If the Ministry does not respond within three months of the appeal being filed, this constitutes a silencio administrativo (administrative silence) — which, in the case of visa appeals, is treated as a tacit refusal. You can then proceed to a judicial appeal on this basis.

If the administrative appeal is rejected (or treated as rejected by administrative silence), you can escalate to the Spanish administrative courts. This is a formal judicial process before the Juzgado Central de lo Contencioso-Administrativo in Madrid — the court that has jurisdiction over consular decisions. You have two months from the final administrative decision to file.

A judicial appeal requires a Spanish-registered lawyer (abogado) and a court representative (procurador). This is not optional — without both, the case cannot be heard. The judicial appeal examines whether the administration's decision was lawful — it is not an opportunity to simply submit better documents. The court asks: did the consulate correctly apply the law? Did it follow due process?

This route is most effective where there is a clear procedural error or a demonstrable misapplication of the law. It is time-consuming and expensive — but where the alternative is losing your study opportunity entirely and the case is strong, it may be the correct path.

What Constitutes Valid Grounds for Appeal?

An appeal is not a second chance to ask nicely. It must identify a specific legal or procedural error in the consulate's decision. Valid grounds include:

  • Factual error: The consulate reached a conclusion of fact that is demonstrably incorrect — for example, concluding that your financial means were insufficient when, properly calculated under the applicable regulation, they met the threshold.
  • Legal error: The consulate applied the wrong legal standard — for example, applying a more onerous financial test than the law requires, or refusing on a ground that is not specified in the regulations.
  • Procedural error: The consulate failed to follow the correct procedure — for example, did not allow you to correct a minor document deficiency as required before refusing, or failed to notify you of the refusal in the legally required manner.
  • Lack of reasoning: The refusal letter gives no reason, or gives only a formulaic reason that does not engage with the specific circumstances of your application. Spanish administrative law requires individualized reasoning.

Not a valid ground: Disagreeing with the outcome, believing you should have been approved, or wanting to submit better documents. Appeals are legal challenges, not reconsiderations. If your refusal was due to a document problem, reapplying is almost always the right route.

In practice, our lawyers often recommend a dual strategy: file the administrative appeal to preserve your legal rights and create a formal record of the challenge, while simultaneously preparing and submitting a corrected application. This ensures you are not waiting 3–6 months for an appeal outcome while your course start date passes.

Timeline of the Appeal Process

D0

Refusal notification received

The clock starts from this date. Read the refusal letter immediately and note the article of law cited.

D1–7

Consult a lawyer

Brief an immigration lawyer immediately. They will assess whether grounds for appeal exist and advise on the appeal vs reapply decision.

D7–21

Appeal drafted and filed

The recurso de alzada is drafted in Spanish, referencing the specific legal grounds, and filed with the Ministry of Foreign Affairs. This must happen within 1 calendar month of notification.

M1–3

Ministry review period

The Ministry examines the appeal. They may request additional information. No travel to Spain is possible during this period.

M3–6

Decision or administrative silence

The Ministry issues a decision, or 3 months elapse without response (treated as rejection). If rejected, you have 2 months to file a judicial appeal.

M6+

Judicial appeal (if applicable)

If proceeding to court, the process takes an additional 12–24 months. Court costs apply. This stage is only recommended where there is a strong legal case.

What Happens to Your Visa Status While You Wait?

Filing an appeal does not grant you any right to enter or remain in Spain. The refusal decision remains in force throughout the appeal process. You cannot travel to Spain on the basis of a pending appeal. If you have an outstanding application for another type of visa, the appeal does not affect that separately.

If your course has already started by the time your appeal is resolved — which is almost certain given the 3–6 month timeline — your school may hold your place, defer your enrolment, or cancel your registration depending on their policies. Speak to your school's international admissions office as soon as possible after receiving the refusal.

When You Need a Lawyer — and When You Don't

Strictly speaking, you can file a recurso de alzada yourself without legal representation — there is no mandatory requirement for a lawyer at the administrative appeal stage. However, there are strong practical reasons to use one:

  • The appeal must be written in formal Spanish and must correctly identify the legal error in the consulate's reasoning — this requires knowledge of Spanish administrative law
  • An appeal that does not identify specific legal grounds will be dismissed
  • A poorly drafted appeal can actually weaken your position by creating an unfavourable record if you later pursue a judicial appeal
  • An experienced immigration lawyer knows which arguments succeed and which don't at the Ministry level

For a judicial appeal, representation by both an abogado and a procurador is legally mandatory. There are no exceptions.

Cost of Appealing

The recurso de alzada filing itself is free — there is no fee payable to the Spanish government. If you engage a lawyer to draft and file it, professional fees typically range from €300–€800 depending on the complexity of the case and the amount of documentation involved.

A judicial appeal is significantly more expensive. You will need to budget for both an abogado and a procurador. Combined professional fees for a first-instance judicial appeal typically range from €1,500–€4,000+, excluding any court fees. Given this, a judicial appeal should only be pursued where the legal grounds are strong and the outcome of winning — being granted the visa — meaningfully justifies the cost.

Consider: if your course fees are €8,000 and you have already paid a non-refundable deposit of €3,000, spending €1,500–€2,000 on a well-founded judicial appeal may make financial sense. If your course is a 2-week summer language programme, it probably does not.
